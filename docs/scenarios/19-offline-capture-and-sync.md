# 19: Working Without Connectivity

People in the field often work in areas with unreliable or no connectivity. They still need to do their work — recording observations, making decisions, noting what they've done. The work can't wait for a connection.

When connectivity returns, what they recorded needs to be reconciled with what others may have recorded in the meantime. Two people may have independently documented things about the same subject during the same period. Decisions made while disconnected may conflict with decisions made elsewhere.

It must remain clear when the work was actually done, not just when it was received by the central system.

This results in:

* The ability to do meaningful work without connectivity
* Reconciliation of independently recorded information when connectivity resumes
* Handling of situations where different people made conflicting records while disconnected
* A clear distinction between when work was done and when it became centrally visible

---

## What makes this hard

Two people may record conflicting information about the same subject while both are offline — different observations, different assessments, different decisions. Neither knows about the other's work until sync. The conflict may not be detectable automatically; it may require a human judgment that cannot be made by either party alone.

A decision made offline — an approval, a status change, a referral — may conflict with a decision made online or by someone else who synced first. The offline decision was valid when it was made. The question of which takes precedence, or whether both must be reconciled, depends on context that the system cannot fully determine on its own.

The order of work performed offline is meaningful but fragile. A field worker may record observations in a specific sequence, but sync may deliver them in a different order, or interleave them with records from others. The apparent timeline of events — as seen centrally — may not match the actual timeline as experienced in the field.

The duration of disconnection matters. Hours offline is a different problem than days or weeks. The longer the gap, the more likely it is that the central state has changed significantly — new assignments, changed rules, deactivated subjects — and the more work the offline user has done against an increasingly stale understanding of the world.
