# 16: Responding to an Unexpected Crisis

An unexpected event — an outbreak, a recall, a natural disaster — forces immediate action. The situation is urgent and evolving: the scope of what needs attention changes rapidly as new information comes in.

Normal procedures may be too slow. People who wouldn't normally have authority to act may need to step in. Communication must be fast and reach the right people quickly, but not so noisy that important messages get lost in a flood.

Work that was already in progress before the crisis remains relevant for context, but responders need to focus on the latest information without waiting for normal reporting cycles.

This results in:

* Rapid mobilization of effort in response to an unplanned event
* An evolving scope of attention as the situation develops
* Expedited decision-making that may bypass normal procedures
* Fast, targeted communication that avoids overwhelming people
* Access to both current crisis information and relevant prior context
